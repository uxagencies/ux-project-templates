# UX Research Plan
### [Study name]

> [!NOTE]
> **Why this document exists.** A research plan keeps a study tied to a real decision. Before anyone recruits a participant, it forces the question: *what will we do differently depending on what we learn?* If there's no answer, don't run the study — you're collecting reassurance, not evidence.

> [!TIP]
> Treat this as a working agreement with your stakeholders, not a formality. Share the draft and get them to confirm the decision in Section 2 is theirs. Research that answers a question nobody asked gets ignored, however good it is.

## 3. Goals & research questions

> [!NOTE]
> Goals and research questions are different. A *goal* is what you want to achieve ("understand why trial users don't convert"). A *research question* is something a participant's behaviour or words can actually answer.

**Goal(s):**
- [e.g. Understand what stops trial users from upgrading]

**Research questions:**

> [!TIP]
> **Good vs. bad questions.** Anchor them in observable behaviour and past experience, never hypotheticals.
> - Bad: *"Do users like the new design?"* — liking isn't measurable or decision-useful.
> - Bad: *"Would you use this feature?"* — predicts intent, which people are terrible at.
> - Good: *"Where do users hesitate or fail when completing [task]?"*
> - Good: *"What do users expect when they tap [X], and how does that differ from what happens?"*

## 4. Method & rationale

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Method follows from the question, not from habit. Pick the lightest method that can answer it.

**Quick method guide** *(full version in the [cheat sheet](CHEATSHEET.md))*:

| If you want to know… | Consider |
|---|---|
| *Why* people behave a certain way | Interviews, contextual inquiry |
| Whether a design *works* (can people use it?) | Usability testing |
| *How many* / *how often*, at scale | Survey, analytics |
| How people *organise / label* concepts | Card sort, tree test |
| Behaviour *over time* | Diary study |
| Which of two options performs better | A/B test (needs traffic) |

- **Chosen method:** [e.g. Moderated usability test, remote]
- **Why this method:** [How it answers the questions above]
- **Format:** [Remote / in-person, moderated / unmoderated, tool used]

## 5. Participants

> [!TIP]
> **How many?** For *qualitative usability* testing, ~5 participants per distinct user group surfaces most major issues — add a round rather than a crowd. For *quantitative* work (surveys, benchmarking) you need a sample large enough to be statistically meaningful, which is a different order of magnitude. Match the number to the method, not the calendar.

## 7. Ethics, consent & data handling

> [!WARNING]
> This isn't bureaucracy — it's how you keep participants' trust and stay on the right side of privacy law. If any participants are in the EU/EEA, **GDPR applies regardless of where your company sits.**

- [ ] Participants give **informed consent** before starting (purpose, recording, right to stop anytime)
- [ ] Recordings and personal data are **stored securely** and access-limited
- [ ] Data is **anonymised / pseudonymised** in any shared output
- [ ] A **retention and deletion** plan exists (how long recordings are kept, when deleted)
- [ ] Participants know **how to withdraw** their data after the session
